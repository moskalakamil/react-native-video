Custom Controls

The default player controls that ship with AVPlayer (iOS) and ExoPlayer (Android) are functional, but they're a black box. You can't restyle them, you can't move them, and you can't extend them without fighting the platform. For many production applications, that's not enough.

This guide covers when and how to build your own video controls - and what you'll run into along the way. react-native-video doesn't ship a custom controls UI out of the box. If chapters with seekbar markers are what you need, we can integrate that quickly - see the Chapters tab for what that looks like. Everything else described here is yours to build, or ours to build for you.

Why Custom Controls?​

Custom video UI comes up more often than you'd expect:

  • Design system alignment - your app has a visual identity; the default controls don't follow it
  • Custom overlays - live chat, polling, ranked leaderboards, or annotations that sit on top of or around the player
  • Control placement - controls below the player (not overlaid), controls in the navigation bar, or persistent controls that don't auto-hide
  • Thumbnail on seek - showing a frame preview while scrubbing; not available natively on either platform
  • Cross-platform consistency - custom controls can share the same design language across iOS and Android, while still respecting each platform's native conventions to keep interactions feeling smooth and familiar on both

Keeping the Native Feeling​

Building custom controls doesn't mean giving up native capabilities - and that's exactly the point. The video is still rendered by AVPlayer on iOS and ExoPlayer on Android, the same engines Apple and Google use in their own apps. Your controls sit on top, but the underlying player is fully native.

This matters for features you simply can't get in a WebView-based player. Picture-in-Picture, background audio, and system media controls are OS-level integrations that aren't accessible from a WebView - they only work because the underlying player is native. Custom controls preserve all of that.

But native feeling goes beyond features - and it comes from two places at once. Some expectations are set by the platform itself (AVPlayer, ExoPlayer), others by apps like YouTube or Netflix that have effectively become the new standard. Users don't distinguish between the two. If they're used to double-tapping to seek, or holding to speed up playback, they'll expect it in your app too - regardless of whether it originated from a native API or a custom player someone built years ago. The best implementations borrow from both, picking whichever pattern users have already internalized.

Users notice it in the details:

  • Double-tap seek with accumulated jumps - tapping the left or right side of the player skips backward or forward. Consecutive taps accumulate: two taps = 20 seconds, three = 30. Each tap triggers an animated ripple showing the total. Apps like YouTube and Netflix set this expectation and users bring it everywhere.
  • Swipe for brightness and volume - vertical swipe on the left half of the player controls screen brightness, right half controls volume. The spatial split is intuitive but calls for careful gesture zone detection and visual feedback (a HUD that appears without blocking the content).
  • Variable scrubbing speed via vertical drag - on iOS native players, dragging the thumb upward while scrubbing slows the scrub rate: 1× at baseline, ½× higher up, ¼× further, and fine scrubbing at the top. This is entirely invisible until you discover it, but once you do, imprecise seeking feels painful without it.
  • Controls auto-hide at exactly the right moment - 2 seconds of inactivity on mobile. Not 1.5, not 3. Too fast feels aggressive, too slow clutters the content. And the return must be instant on any touch - no debounce, no delay.
  • Haptic feedback on meaningful events - a subtle tap when scrubbing past a chapter marker, or when hitting the beginning or end of a video. iOS native players do this. Apps that skip it feel slightly hollow in comparison.
  • Backward seeking feels different from forward - seeking backward through a remote stream is inherently choppier due to keyframe distribution in the encoded video. Native players compensate with visual strategies (fade, lower opacity during seek) so it doesn't feel like a bug. Ignoring this makes backward seeking feel broken.

Visual language​

Each platform has its own visual conventions for media controls, and matching them is part of what makes an app feel right.

On iOS, that means adopting Liquid Glass - the translucent, fluid material introduced in iOS 26. Apple's own media controls use it, and users increasingly expect it in polished apps. Reproducing it faithfully with BlurView and the right border/opacity treatment goes a long way toward feeling at home on the platform.

On Android, Material You theming and the platform's motion curves are the equivalent baseline.

Platform-specific interaction patterns​

Beyond visuals, each platform has subtle behavioral conventions that users have internalized without realizing it.

On iOS, when controls are auto-hidden, tapping the area where the play/pause button sits doesn't just reveal the controls - it also toggles playback at the same time. Users expect this. If your implementation shows the controls on first tap and requires a second tap to play or pause, it feels broken even if nothing is technically wrong.

Android behaves differently - a tap anywhere typically surfaces the controls, and interaction follows as a second step. Building for both platforms means handling these interaction models separately, not assuming one pattern fits both.

That's the goal worth aiming for: custom, but native.

The Right Architecture: Compound Components​

The most common mistake when building custom video controls is managing state in the wrong place - passing isPlaying, currentTime, duration, and a handful of callbacks as props through multiple component layers. This approach breaks quickly as complexity grows.

The community-proven pattern is Compound Components - a design pattern where a parent component owns shared state via React Context, and child components compose freely without prop chains.

Fernando Rojo gave a great talk on this at a React Universe conference: Composition Is All You Need. Highly recommended before starting.

The API you're building toward looks like this:

<VideoPlayer>
  <VideoPlayer.Video />
  <VideoPlayer.Controls>
    <VideoPlayer.PlayButton />
    <VideoPlayer.SeekBar />
    <VideoPlayer.FullscreenButton />
  </VideoPlayer.Controls>
</VideoPlayer>

VideoPlayer maintains a Context with:

  • isPlaying, currentTime, duration, buffered, volume
  • play(), pause(), seek(timeSeconds), setVolume(value)

Each child component reads only what it needs from that context. You can rearrange, replace, or skip any control independently.

Getting Started​

Step 1: Disable native controls

<VideoView
  player={player}
  controls={false}
/>

Step 2: Create the PlayerContext

Wire VideoView callbacks into a Context Provider. At a minimum, onProgress, onLoad, and onPlaybackStateChanged will cover most use cases. Your context value becomes the single source of truth.

Step 3: Build the smallest possible control

A Play/Pause button consuming context is a good first milestone. If that renders correctly and responds to player state, your context wiring is correct. Everything else follows the same pattern.

At this point things will likely feel fine. The real performance issues tend to surface later - as the number of controls grows, especially more advanced ones, unnecessary re-renders start to accumulate. A context update that triggers the entire control tree to re-render on every player event is the most common culprit. It's worth keeping an eye on the render count early rather than debugging jank later.

One effective way to avoid this for progress-driven UI is to use react-native-reanimated shared values. Instead of storing currentTime in React state, the playback position lives as a SharedValue that updates directly on the UI thread.

What to Build​

Basic controls (JS-only)​

These are achievable without native modules:

  • Play / Pause button - straightforward context consumer
  • Skip ±10s buttons - calls seek(currentTime ± 10)
  • Time display - formats currentTime and duration
  • Fullscreen button - triggers orientation lock and layout change

The seekbar​

The seekbar looks simple. It isn't.

A production seekbar typically includes:

  • A progress layer (current playback position)
  • A buffered layer (how much has loaded)
  • A draggable thumb with gesture handling
  • Smooth animation during playback (not jumpy with each onProgress tick)
  • Debounced seek - updating the player on every gesture frame is too aggressive
  • Gesture responder coordination with parent ScrollView components

Using react-native-reanimated for the animation layer makes smooth performance significantly more achievable - without it, the thumb tends to visibly stutter on mid-range devices. Even with Reanimated, expect to spend time debugging "jumping" - where the thumb snaps back momentarily as the native onProgress event fires during a drag.

For iOS chapters, marker positions can be calculated as percentages of total duration and rendered as dots on the track - accounting for thumb size so markers don't end up hidden underneath it.

Advanced controls​

Volume slider On iOS, hardware volume is controlled via MPVolumeView - system volume isn't accessible from JS directly. On Android, AudioManager is reachable via a native bridge. Many teams skip the volume slider altogether or reach for a native wrapper library.

Resolution / quality selector Switching quality isn't just calling setSource(). On iOS, HLS quality selection goes through AVPlayer's preferredPeakBitRate. On Android, you manipulate ExoPlayer's TrackSelector directly. The UI is a menu - but the state behind it (available tracks, current selection, switching behavior) is non-trivial. The player also needs to handle the brief interruption during quality change.

Subtitle selector Custom subtitle rendering means displaying timed text on an overlay. You lose all the native positioning, styling, and encoding support that AVPlayer and ExoPlayer provide for free. Expect edge cases around RTL text, multi-line captions, and timing drift.

AirPlay (iOS) AirPlay works by wrapping Apple's native MPVolumeView. The button appearance is constrained - you're embedding a native view, not rendering a custom one. It also won't show up in the iOS simulator. A library like react-native-airplay-ios handles the bridging.

Chromecast Chromecast works through the Google Cast SDK, which means native integration on both platforms. Discovery, connection state, and playback control all go through the SDK. react-native-google-cast wraps this for React Native - plan for some platform setup time before getting to actual testing.

Picture-in-Picture iOS and Android have entirely different PiP APIs, activation conditions, and lifecycle behaviors. iOS PiP goes through AVPictureInPictureController; Android uses enterPictureInPictureMode() with aspect ratio hints. The React Native bridge for both involves careful handling of background/foreground transitions.

What Teams Consistently Underestimate​

The seekbar that looks like 50 lines ends up being 500 when you handle all edge cases. AirPlay and Chromecast aren't JavaScript problems - they involve native bridging on both platforms. Resolution switching is a state machine, not a prop. Fullscreen means hiding the status bar and navigation bar, locking orientation, and expanding the layout edge-to-edge.

Custom subtitle rendering means reimplementing what the platform provides for free - with support for positioning, styling, timing, and encoding. And all of this needs to work while the app is backgrounded, during interruptions (calls, notifications), and on devices from three years ago.
